Sensorised robot limbs

A short essay for non-roboticists

08 August 2018 Patrick van der Smagt

The below gives a very succinct overview of some main issues to robotics—but be forewarned that it mainly deals with robotic arms (or, arguably, other limbs).

Traditional robot arms

A large part of traditional robotics can be described as transformations of manifolds. For instance, a pick-and-place operation can be formulated as follows:

  1. The pixel image \(I\) is transformed to Cartesian coordinates \((x,y,z)\) indicating, e.g., the centre of mass of the known object;
  2. The target position \(\boldsymbol p_d=(x,y,z)\) is transformed to a set of joint angles \(\boldsymbol q_d\) that describe the joint angles the robot should take so that its end-effector reach the position \(\boldsymbol p_d\);
  3. The desired joint angles \(\boldsymbol q_d\) is transformed to a sequence of joint angles \(\boldsymbol q_0, \boldsymbol q_0 + \Delta\boldsymbol q, \boldsymbol q_0 + 2\Delta\boldsymbol q, \dots, \boldsymbol q_d\), describing a trajectory in joint space;
  4. \(\Delta\boldsymbol q\) is transformed to joint torques \(\boldsymbol\tau\);
  5. \(\boldsymbol\tau\) is transformed to motor currents \(\boldsymbol i\).

Each and every one of the abovementioned transformations is defined by a model. For instance, in the first transformation this is an environment model, in which the target object is known (and thus can be recognised through an algorithm), while the camera parameters are also known (or calibrated, and thus the pixel image can be transformed to a coordinate in Cartesian space). In the second transformation, Cartesian space can be transformed to joint space through inverse kinematics, requiring detailed knowledge of the robot system, while at the same time possibly solving underdeterminedness of the system.

The use of such models is both advantageous and a problem. When the models are highly accurate—e.g., the forwards and cq. inverse kinematics of a robot arm—as well as stable, and can be easily obtained, their use to linearise the transformations is efficient. But in some cases this is not easily done. Already the first transformation in the above example demonstrates this: it limits the system to work in predefined environments only, and restricts its use to simple, highly dependable and calibrated sensors. When, e.g., cameras are not calibrated, or soft tactile sensors are used, models do not suffice or are unavailable, and machine-learning methods are needed. Or, increase the complexity of the task and models are insufficient. This happens, for instance, when the goal cannot be simply described

Similarly problematic is the case there the dynamics of the robot is no longer simply computed (step above). And this is where industrial and modern robotics diverge. The dynamics of an industrial robot can be simplified by assuming independence of each of its joints. That is, the movement of joint \(i\) is assumed to have no influence on that of joints \(i-1\) nor \(i+1\)—or any of the other joints for that matter. By building the robot's (direct-drive or low gear ratio) motors strong enough so that this assumption holds, and making its beams (links) strong and non-bending, this approach works well. But the resulting robot is so strong, that interaction with humans is too dangerous.

Light-weight robots

To solve this, light-weight robots increased in popularity in the 1990s. The principle of such a robot is simple: apart from a light structure, the light-weight robot is defined by small, high-revolute motors which can only create useful torques through high-ratio gears (e.g., 1:100; typically a harmonic gear). The whole structure can be built at rather low weights—a reduction by an order of magnitude is possible while reaching the same torques—but introduces some problems:

  1. The nonlinearity of the gears are hard to model, thus leading to inaccuracies;
  2. The high gear ratio introduces backlash;
  3. The reduced strength of the beams introduces undesired flexibilities;
  4. The high gear ratio reduces the maximal speed and acceleration of the joints.

Though models, in combination with feedback control, can still be constructed to solve the former of the above problems, still it requires very accurate robot production methods—in particular for the gear—thus increasing the cost of the robot. This was observed in, e.g., the KUKA LWR, which was about an order of magnitude more expensive than its "stiff" counterparts.

The second problem is harder to counteract. In a harmonic gear this is rather a matter of back drive: a small movement at the motor side has no measurable effect at the link side. The DLR LWR-III, later sold as KUKA LWR-4 and KUKA iiwa, solves this elegantly by including joint torque sensors at the link side. This has the advantage that the joints can be used as sensors, since models of joint and gears allows measuring differences between expected and the measured torques, thus enabling relatively accurate measurement of outside impacts. The sensors, of course, increase costs, but the system now can be used for impedance control. This control strategy, inspired by biological behaviour, is important for interaction of robots with the environment. Rather than defining the position of the joint, it defines its position and stiffness. The latter can be seen as a force field or spring around the position; the characteristics of these springs can be defined by control.

From March 2009 the KUKA LBR 4 has been used in the fully automated assembly of rear axle gearboxes at the Mercedes-Benz Untertürkheim plant, assembling hundreds of thousands of gear boxes. Its "sensitivity" allow it to be used in assembly operations, quite comparable to how humans would do it.

But it also introduces a problem: apart from the joint position, a robot movement now also needs to define a joint stiffness along its trajectory. How is this best chosen? Two approaches are possible: (1) by trying to observe humans using their stiffness variation, or (2) by optimal control.

The former of these approaches is problematic: not only is measuring stiffness in human behaviour very hard, esp. during movement; but also, the range of stiffness that humans can attain and at what cost differs dramatically from robots. A transfer of strategies may lead to high inefficiency.

Optimal-control strategies suffer from two other problems. First, traditional optimal control is non-tractable in the general case; that currently already occurs with robots with 2 degrees of freedom. Second, it is not always clear which cost function an optimal-control algorithm should optimise.

Finally, problem above is serious, as it limits the efficiency of a robot. If a robot moves or reacts slower than a human, it is no longer "fun" to work with it. Waiting for a machine is frustrating, and leads to reduced acceptability of such.

Variable-impedance robots

Working with the impedance-control subject shortly explained in the previous section, each position of the robot—be it in Cartesian or joint space—can be seen as having an additional mechanical spring connected to it. The robot can be moved away from a position through an external influence, and the force needed to do so is linearly related to how far that deflection is.

But the spring is only defined by control. It is not a real spring, and therefore cannot store energy. Looking again into biology, there's something to be said to use real springs. Not only could these be used to absorb energy in the case of an impact, but also could that stored energy be used to generate large torques and thus very swift movements—the kangaroo-effect, so to say.

Variable-stiffness cq. variable-impedance robots implement this in hardware, by adding additional springs to each actuators. In order to be variable, the spring constants of these must be changeable. Furthermore, since springs are physically finite, they must be nonlinear. Finally, of course springs add oscillations, so the control of the joints need to take damping into account—in fact, not different from the robots in the previous section.

Possible implementation of a nonlinear spring, as needed in a variable-impedance robot. The stiffness is linearly related to \(l / h\).

Such robots not being universally available for commercial applications, they do have their merits, solving most if not all of the problems mentioned above. But of course, some problems are also introduced:

  • The mechanical construction is difficult, and currently not ripe;
  • Each joint now has 3 control parameters: position, stiffness, and damping, thus making optimal control more complex;
  • Models of the joints are very difficult to obtain.

To our current knowledge, the above problems can only be used via modern machine-learning approaches.

Human-robot interaction

Both the abovementioned light-weight robots and the variable-impedance robots are, in principle, suitable for human–robot interaction. Neither system is strong enough for serious peril—e.g., the DLR LWR-III was measured at a HIC of around 25. That makes the robot good to interact with humans. But can the human interact with the robot? To do so, the robot must be an acceptable proxy for another human. This requires various levels of interaction:

  • the robot should match human movement agility—possible, perhaps, for the variable-impedance robots mentioned above;
  • the robot should predict human movement, to interact;
  • the robot should recognise human movement.

None of these are easily possible without modern machine-learning approaches.
